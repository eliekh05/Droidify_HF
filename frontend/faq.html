FAQ

20 questions about Droidify and the world of custom Android

About Droidify

1. What is Droidify?

A live index of Android custom ROMs, recoveries, devices, and tools. Think of it as a search engine specifically for the Android modding ecosystem — it pulls data in real time from over 20 public sources so you can find what's available for your device without hunting across a dozen different websites. Nothing is hosted here. Nothing is hardcoded. It's just a very well-connected aggregator.

2. Who built this and why?

A solo developer who got tired of the state of Android ROM discovery. Finding a working ROM for a specific device used to mean opening XDA threads from 2019, clicking dead SourceForge links, checking three different recovery project websites, and eventually stumbling on something that might work — after hours of searching. Droidify is the tool that should have existed before all that.

3. Is Droidify free?

Completely. No account, no subscription, no paywall, no ads. The source code is open on GitHub under the MIT license. You can self-host it on your own machine if you want — the whole thing runs in two Docker containers with a single install command.

4. Where does the data come from?

Directly from the source. LineageOS Download API, OrangeFox API, TWRP's search index, crDroid's website, SourceForge file listings for 33+ ROM projects, GrapheneOS releases, postmarketOS on GitLab, DivestOS, CalyxOS, /e/OS, Ubuntu Touch, Kali NetHunter, and more. Everything is fetched live and cached for up to two hours. No data is manually entered or maintained.

5. Is Droidify affiliated with LineageOS, OrangeFox, or any ROM project?

Not at all. Droidify is an independent project with no ties to any ROM team, recovery project, or Android community organization. It reads their public APIs the same way any browser would. Every trademark mentioned on this site belongs to its respective owner.

Data & Devices

6. Why does my device show zero ROMs?

Several possibilities. Your device might only have builds hosted on Google Drive links in XDA threads — those can't be indexed automatically because there's no structured API. Or the cache is cold and the ROM hasn't appeared yet (wait two minutes after a fresh server start). Or your device genuinely has no active ROM support from any of the projects Droidify indexes. If you believe a ROM exists that should be showing, open a GitHub issue with the codename and the project URL.

7. Why is the first page load slow?

On a fresh server start, the backend fetches data from 20+ external sources simultaneously. This warmup takes roughly two minutes to complete. Once the cache is hot, every page and API call responds instantly. Subsequent restarts load from a saved cache on disk, so the warmup is much faster. The slowness you see is a one-time cost per deployment, not an ongoing issue.

8. How often is the data updated?

Every two hours for most sources. ROM indexes, recovery lists, and device data are all cached with a two-hour TTL. Stock firmware links (SamFW) are cached for 24 hours since they change rarely. If a new ROM build was released an hour ago, it might not appear yet — but it will on the next cache refresh.

9. Can I trust the download links?

Droidify links to the official download pages of each ROM project — SourceForge, the ROM's own website, OrangeFox's CDN, TWRP's server. We don't proxy, mirror, or modify anything. That said, always verify the SHA256 checksum of what you download against what the project publishes. The source name is shown next to every link so you always know exactly where you're going.

10. Can I request a missing ROM or device?

Yes. Open an issue on GitHub with the device codename and the ROM project's public download URL or API endpoint. If the project has a structured file listing or JSON API, it can be added as a scraper source. Single-file Google Drive links or forum posts can't be indexed automatically.

Custom ROMs

11. What is a custom ROM?

A complete replacement for your phone's operating system, built from Android's open source code by a community or team independent of your manufacturer. Custom ROMs can bring newer Android versions to devices the manufacturer abandoned, remove bloatware, add customization options, improve battery life, or offer privacy features not present in stock firmware. LineageOS, crDroid, GrapheneOS, and /e/OS are some of the most well-known examples.

12. What is a GSI ROM?

GSI stands for Generic System Image. It's a ROM build designed to run on any Android device that supports Project Treble — which is most phones launched since 2018. Instead of being compiled specifically for your device's hardware, a GSI uses generic drivers and relies on the device's vendor partition for hardware support. They're great for testing or running on devices with no device-specific ROM available, but may have hardware quirks like a broken camera or missing fingerprint support.

13. What's the difference between official and unofficial builds?

An official build is maintained by the ROM project itself — it goes through their review process, gets listed on their download page, and typically receives ongoing OTA updates. An unofficial build is made by an individual developer outside of the project, often for devices the project doesn't officially support. Unofficial builds can be excellent, but they may be abandoned, less tested, or missing features. Always check when a build was last updated before flashing.

14. Will I lose my data when flashing a ROM?

Almost certainly yes, at least on a clean install. Flashing a ROM typically requires wiping the data partition, which removes all your apps, settings, photos stored internally, and accounts. Back up everything to an external drive or cloud storage before you start. Photos on an SD card are usually safe, but don't rely on it — back those up too.

Bootloader & Safety

15. Will flashing a ROM void my warranty?

Almost certainly yes. Unlocking the bootloader is a prerequisite for flashing any custom ROM, and it permanently changes a flag in your device's firmware. On Samsung devices, this trips Knox — a hardware-level security flag that can never be reset, even if you restore stock firmware completely. Some manufacturers are more lenient than others, but in general: once unlocked, your warranty is gone.

16. What is a bootloader and why does it need to be unlocked?

The bootloader is firmware that runs before Android starts. It verifies that the operating system hasn't been tampered with before loading it. By default it only allows signed, manufacturer-approved software to run — which is why you can't just copy a custom ROM onto your phone and reboot. Unlocking it removes that signature check, letting you load any software you choose. The unlock process itself typically wipes your device.

17. My device bricked — what now?

A soft brick (bootloop, stuck on logo, recovery won't boot) is usually fixable by flashing stock firmware via fastboot. A hard brick is rarer and means the device won't enter any recoverable mode at all. Most Qualcomm devices have an emergency download mode (EDL) accessible by holding specific buttons while plugging in USB — this can reflash the device even when nothing else works. MediaTek devices have a similar BROM mode. Search XDA for your exact model and the word "unbrick" — the community has documented procedures for almost every device.

18. What is a custom recovery and do I need one?

A custom recovery (TWRP, OrangeFox, SHRP, PBRP) is a replacement for the minimal recovery environment that ships with your phone. It gives you a touchscreen interface for flashing zip files, creating and restoring full backups (NANDroid), wiping partitions, and mounting storage. You need one to flash most custom ROMs — though some ROMs (like GrapheneOS) have their own web-based installer that doesn't require a custom recovery at all.

Miscellaneous

19. What is root access and do I need it?

Root access means having superuser (administrator) privileges over your own phone — the ability to read and write any file, install system-level modifications, run ad blockers at the network level, and generally do things the manufacturer decided you shouldn't be able to do. You don't need root to run a custom ROM — LineageOS for example works perfectly without it. But if you want to use Magisk, KernelSU, or Xposed-style mods, root is the prerequisite.

20. Will Google Pay and banking apps still work after flashing?

It depends on the ROM and your setup. Google Pay and most banking apps use Play Integrity (formerly SafetyNet) to verify the device is unmodified. A stock-based ROM without root will usually pass. A ROM with root, or with an unlocked bootloader showing in the attestation, will often fail. Magisk has a hide feature that can sometimes bypass this check, but it's a moving target as Google tightens the requirements. GrapheneOS and CalyxOS both have good Play Integrity support. On LineageOS it varies by device and build. Check the ROM's own documentation before assuming banking will work.
